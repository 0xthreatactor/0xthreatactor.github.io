How Police Track You Through Your Phone — Inside India's Telecom Investigation System

From Tower Dumps to IPDR to CEIR — everything the law enforcement ecosystem uses, explained clearly.

● 18 min read Digital Forensics India Context

In 2012, after the Nirbhaya case, investigators used Call Detail Records (CDRs) to trace the movement of accused individuals across Delhi. In the 2008 Mumbai attacks, telecom intercepts were among the most critical pieces of evidence. In hundreds of kidnapping and murder cases across India, tower dumps have narrowed 10,000-suspect lists down to a handful of leads in hours.

Yet most people — including journalists, lawyers, and students — have a blurry, half-accurate understanding of how this actually works. This guide changes that.

Section 01

The Core Data Types Police Can Access

Before diving into investigation techniques, you need to understand exactly what data exists and what each type tells investigators. India's telecom ecosystem generates five major record types.

Call Detail Records (CDR)

The backbone of telecom investigation. Every call and SMS generates a CDR entry stored by the operator.

  • Calling & receiving number
  • Date, time, and call duration
  • Cell ID (which tower and sector handled the call)
  • IMEI of both devices
  • IMSI (SIM identifier)
  • Call type: voice, SMS, roaming
Response time: 6hrs–3 days   Retention: 2 years (TRAI mandate)
Live / Recent Location Data

Separate from CDR — this is the last known tower connection of an active device. Used in emergencies.

  • Last connected Cell ID
  • Timestamp of last activity
  • Network registration status (online/offline)
  • Latitude/longitude (if operator has GPS-assisted tower mapping)
Response time: 5–60 mins   Use: missing persons, active pursuit
IP Detail Records (IPDR)

The internet equivalent of CDR. Every data session your phone initiates is logged.

  • Source and destination IP addresses
  • Session start and end time
  • Data volume transferred
  • Port numbers (can indicate app type)
  • Cell ID at time of session
  • NAT (Network Address Translation) mapping
Critical for: cybercrime, OTT app tracking   Retention: 1 year mandated
SDR (Subscriber Detail Records) / CAF

This is the identity layer — who owns the SIM? In India, SIM verification is among the most rigorous in the world.

  • Full name, address, date of birth
  • Aadhaar number (mandatory since 2017)
  • Biometric verification record
  • Multiple SIMs issued to same Aadhaar (max 9 in India)
  • Photograph taken at activation
  • Retailer ID who activated the SIM
India advantage: Aadhaar-linking makes fake IDs nearly impossible
Lawful Interception (Content)

Actual call recordings and SMS content — requires much higher authorization than metadata.

  • Requires order under IT Act Section 69 or Indian Telegraph Act Section 5(2)
  • Must be authorized by Home Secretary (Central or State)
  • Subject to strict review every 60 days
  • Does NOT work on end-to-end encrypted apps (WhatsApp, Signal) without device access
  • All operators in India must route through government's CMS nodes
Highest legal threshold — rare in routine crime
Section 02

How Mobile Towers Actually Work — Beyond the Basics

Every explanation of towers stops at "towers have sectors." Here's what that actually means in practice — and what most guides miss.

The Sector System

A standard tower divides its coverage into 3 directional sectors, each covering 120°. Each sector has its own antenna pointing in a specific azimuth direction (0° = North, 120° = Southeast, 240° = Southwest etc.). The Cell ID in a CDR tells you exactly which sector a call was handled by.

Real Example
If a CDR shows Cell ID: MUM-400001-2-B, investigators know: Tower MUM-400001, Sector 2 (120°–240° coverage arc), carrier B. They can now draw a wedge on a map and say: "the suspect was somewhere in this direction during this call."

The "Nearest Tower" Misconception

Your phone doesn't always connect to the nearest tower. It connects to the tower with the strongest signal and least interference. In urban India, due to signal reflections off buildings, a phone in Andheri might connect to a tower in Juhu or Goregaon. This introduces errors in location inference — a critical point for investigators and defense lawyers alike.

What Investigators Know From Cell ID Alone

📍

Tower GPS coordinates

Every Cell ID maps to a known latitude/longitude in operator databases.

⚙

Sector direction

Azimuth tells which compass direction the phone was from the tower.

📶

Tower type & height

Ground-level vs rooftop tower affects the coverage footprint shape.

🛰

Operator & network type

4G towers have smaller cells than 3G/2G — affects area estimate.

Section 03

Tower Dump — India's Most Powerful (and Controversial) Tool

This is the technique that most guides never mention — yet it is one of the most frequently used investigative methods in India for serious crimes.

"A tower dump can give you 50,000 phone numbers. The art of investigation is reducing that number to one."

What is a Tower Dump?

A tower dump is a request to an operator to provide the records of every device that connected to a specific tower (or set of towers) during a specific time window. It is not targeted to a suspect — it is a mass data collection from everyone in an area.

How Tower Dumps Are Used in Indian Investigations

01

Crime location identified

A robbery, murder, or kidnapping occurs. Scene location is confirmed.

02

Time window established

Based on FIR, witnesses, CCTV timestamps — a time bracket is defined (e.g., 9:30–11:45 PM).

03

Tower dump requested from all operators

All operators — Jio, Airtel, Vi, BSNL — are sent requests for every device connecting to towers covering the crime area during that window.

04

Raw list received — tens of thousands of numbers

Initial list can have 20,000 to 100,000 entries depending on area density.

05

First filter — eliminate local residents

Numbers that appear repeatedly on the same tower over weeks are likely residents/commuters, not suspects.

06

Second filter — new or unusual devices

Look for phones that appeared in the area for the first time, or only during the crime window.

07

Cross-reference with witness descriptions, prior records

Check remaining numbers against criminal databases, and subscriber records for names/addresses. Ground investigation follows to narrow further.

Civil Liberties Concern
Tower dumps collect data on thousands of innocent people who happened to be in the area. There is currently no comprehensive legal framework in India specifically regulating tower dump usage — police request them under general provisions of CrPC or IT Act. The Privacy judgment (Puttaswamy, 2017) is the most significant check, but lacks implementing legislation as of 2024.

The "Common Number" Technique

One of the most powerful refinements: investigators pull tower dumps from multiple crime scenes (if a serial offender is suspected) and use set intersection — finding numbers that appear in the dumps of all crime locations. If the same IMEI or number appears near 3 different crime scenes at the right times, the probability of coincidence collapses rapidly.

Section 04

IPDR — The Internet Trail Most People Don't Know Exists

As India shifted to 4G/5G and Indians moved from voice calls to WhatsApp, Telegram, and data-driven communication, the CDR became less useful for some categories of crime. IPDR filled the gap.

What IPDR Records

Every time your phone opens an internet connection — whether to send a WhatsApp message, browse Instagram, or connect to any server — the operator's system logs an IPDR entry:

# Sample IPDR structure (what investigators receive) { timestamp_start: "2024-08-14 22:17:43", timestamp_end: "2024-08-14 22:18:01", msisdn: "91-XXXXXXXXXX", # SIM number imei: "35XXXXXXXXXX", src_ip: "10.x.x.x", # internal carrier IP dst_ip: "157.240.x.x", # e.g., Facebook/Meta server dst_port: 443, # HTTPS — encrypted content cell_id: "MUM-400072-1-A", nat_ip: "49.36.x.x", # external IP assigned bytes_up: 2048, bytes_down: 18432 }
Why This Matters
Even though WhatsApp uses end-to-end encryption and the content of messages is inaccessible, IPDR reveals: which server was contacted, when, from which tower, and how much data was exchanged. Cybercrime investigators use this to prove a device was communicating with a specific platform at a specific time and place — without needing the message content.

IPDR in Cybercrime Investigation

For online fraud cases, IPDR is often the primary evidence chain. If a victim reports receiving a fraud call from a number that used a WhatsApp business account, investigators use:

Victim's CDR → call received at time T → Suspect's IPDR → data connection to WhatsApp servers at time T → Cell ID from IPDR → location of suspect during call → SDR → identity of subscriber.

TRAI / DoT Mandate
All licensed telecom operators in India are required under license conditions to maintain IPDR for at least 1 year and make it available to authorized law enforcement. CERT-In additionally mandated in 2022 that all service providers maintain logs for 180 days and provide them within 6 hours of a government request.
Section 05

IMEI Tracking & India's CEIR — The Device Fingerprint System

The IMEI (International Mobile Equipment Identity) is a 15-digit unique identifier baked into every mobile device at manufacture. Unlike SIM cards — which can be changed — the IMEI identifies the physical handset.

What IMEI Enables

📱

Device tracking across SIM changes

Even if a criminal swaps SIM cards, the same IMEI keeps appearing in CDR and IPDR records, linking activity to one device.

🚫

Stolen phone blacklisting

When a phone is reported stolen, its IMEI is blacklisted — preventing it from connecting to any Indian network.

🔍

Tracing device purchase

IMEI is registered at point of sale. Investigators can trace which shop sold a device and obtain CCTV / purchase records.

CEIR — Central Equipment Identity Register

India launched CEIR (Central Equipment Identity Register) under the DoT as a national IMEI database. It is one of the most significant additions to India's telecom investigation infrastructure in recent years.

2017
IMEI tampering made a criminal offence under the Indian Telegraph Act. Changing or cloning IMEI carries up to 3 years imprisonment.
2019
CEIR pilot launched in Maharashtra, Karnataka, and Rajasthan — allowing stolen phone reporting and network-wide blocking.
2021
National rollout of CEIR with citizen portal at ceir.gov.in for anyone to check or report phone status.
2023
Telecom Act 2023 passed — strengthened IMEI regulations and created statutory basis for device identity management.
2024
Sanchar Saathi portal expansion — DoT's citizen tool allows checking SIMs registered on Aadhaar, blocking lost phones, and IMEI verification.
Sanchar Saathi
Any Indian citizen can now visit sancharsaathi.gov.in to see how many SIM cards are registered against their Aadhaar, disconnect unauthorized SIMs, and report/block a stolen phone's IMEI — all services that didn't exist before 2021.

The Legal Framework — What Law Allows Police to Access

India's telecom investigation powers are drawn from a patchwork of legislation. Here's a clear map of what authorizes what:

Law / Section What it authorizes Who can authorize Notes
CrPC § 91
now BNSS § 94		 Production of documents / records from any person or organization Any Magistrate or Police Officer Used for CDR, IPDR, SDR requests. Most common route.
CrPC § 92 Postal/electronic records from postal services Session Court or above Used for email records from service providers
IT Act § 69 Interception, monitoring, or decryption of computer/communication data Central/State Home Secretary Highest threshold — for actual content access
Indian Telegraph Act § 5(2) Interception of messages on public emergency or security grounds Central/State Home Secretary 100+ year old law still active — being consolidated into Telecom Act 2023

Important Gap
India currently does not have a comprehensive data protection law specific to surveillance. The Digital Personal Data Protection Act (DPDPA) 2023 was passed but explicitly exempts national security and law enforcement use. This means telecom data access by police has fewer procedural guardrails than comparable democracies like Germany, UK, or Canada.
Section 07

CMS & NATGRID — India's Surveillance Architecture

Centralized Monitoring System (CMS)

The CMS is a government-operated infrastructure that allows authorized agencies to directly access telecom content and metadata without going through the operator's manual processes for each request. Built by C-DOT (Centre for Development of Telematics), it was made operational around 2013.

📡

Direct operator tap-in

All licensed operators in India must install CMS nodes that give designated agencies direct access under authorized orders.

📌

No operator visibility

When CMS access is used, the operator is not notified — only the government system is informed of the interception.

🏢

Central & state access

Both central agencies (IB, RAW, CBI, NIA) and state police can request CMS access through their designated nodal officers.

NATGRID

National Intelligence Grid (NATGRID) is a separate, connected-databases project that links 21 sets of databases from agencies across the Indian government — including:

Telecom records · Immigration data · Tax records · Bank transaction data · Railway ticketing · Airline ticketing · Vehicle registration · Criminal records · Visa records

While NATGRID was conceptualized post-26/11 and has faced delays, its aim is to allow authorized intelligence officers to run a single query that crosses data from all these sources simultaneously — turning siloed investigation into integrated analysis.

Who Has Access
Access to CMS and NATGRID is not available to regular state police investigators for everyday cases. It is restricted to designated central agencies and senior officers. A local SHO investigating a robbery would use the standard CDR request process, not CMS.
Section 08

How Police Actually Request Telecom Data in India

Theory is useful. Here is the ground-level operational process followed in most Indian police investigations when requesting CDR/location data:

01

FIR registered, investigating officer assigned

The IO determines that telecom data is needed. In serious cases (murder, kidnapping, terrorism), this happens within hours.

02

Requisition letter drafted to operator

Under CrPC § 91 (now BNSS § 94), the IO sends a formal requisition — on letterhead, with case number — to the operator's Law Enforcement Response Team (LERT).

03

Operators have dedicated LERT teams

Airtel, Jio, Vi, and BSNL all maintain 24/7 Law Enforcement Response Teams with direct hotlines for urgent cases. These are separate from customer service.

04

Urgency classification matters

Operators respond faster for "life at risk" or "kidnapping" classifications — sometimes within 30–60 minutes for location data. Standard CDR: 24–72 hours.

05

Data received — analysis begins

Data arrives as Excel sheets, CSV files, or encrypted PDFs. Officers use specialized software (and increasingly commercial tools like Cellebrite or in-house tools) to process.

06

Court submission requires certificate

For data to be admissible in court, the operator must provide a Section 65B certificate under the Indian Evidence Act — certifying authenticity of electronic records.

Section 65B — Critical for Admissibility
Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act (now Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam) is the gateway for electronic evidence in Indian courts. Telecom data without a proper 65B certificate from the operator is routinely challenged and sometimes excluded. The Supreme Court clarified in Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal (2020) that 65B certificates must be obtained at the time of collection, not after the fact.
Section 09

Triangulation & Geofencing — Advanced Location Techniques

Cell Triangulation

When a phone is in range of multiple towers simultaneously (common in urban India), the operator's system can estimate location using signal timing differences between towers. This is called Time Difference of Arrival (TDOA) triangulation.

The math is straightforward: if Tower A receives the phone's signal 50 nanoseconds before Tower B, and both towers' coordinates are known, the phone must be on a hyperbolic curve defined by that time difference. Three towers give three curves. Their intersection is the estimated location.

Accuracy in India
Triangulation in dense urban India (Mumbai, Delhi, Bengaluru) can achieve 50–150 meter accuracy. In rural areas with sparse towers, it may only achieve 1–5 km accuracy. GPS-assisted towers (used by Jio in many areas) improve this significantly.

Geofencing

Geofencing is the technique of defining a geographic boundary and requesting all device records that appeared within that boundary during a time period. It is essentially a refined, boundary-defined tower dump.

Indian investigators use geofencing in two ways:

Reactive geofencing — after a crime, draw a boundary around the crime scene and pull all records.

Tracking geofencing — for an active suspect, request an alert whenever their device connects to a tower within a defined area.

Movement Path Analysis

This is where CDR becomes a powerful narrative tool. By plotting a suspect's tower connections over time on a map, investigators reconstruct the movement path — which direction they traveled, at what speed, where they stopped, and how their path relates to the crime timeline.

Real-World Example Pattern
Suspect's phone connects: Tower A (9:00 PM, near home) → Tower B (9:22 PM, highway direction) → Tower C (9:45 PM, crime scene area) → Tower D (10:15 PM, exit route) → Tower A (11:30 PM, back home). This pattern, if matching witnesses and CCTV timestamps, builds a compelling case — even without GPS.
Section 10

Challenges, Evasion Tactics & Their Investigative Counters

Sophisticated criminals — and organized syndicates operating in India — are aware of telecom tracking. Here's how evasion is attempted and how investigators respond:

The evasion: Criminals use different SIM cards for different activities, switching frequently.

The counter: IMEI stays constant. Even with 10 different SIMs, the same handset's IMEI appears in CDR, linking all SIM activity to one device. Additionally, Aadhaar-linked SIM limits (max 9 per person, flagged above 3 in many systems) make bulk acquisition harder. Bulk SIM frauds (where agents issued SIMs on fake/stolen Aadhaar) have been significantly reduced since 2023 re-verification drives.
The evasion: Making calls only via WhatsApp/Telegram to avoid CDR trails.

The counter: IPDR captures all data sessions including WhatsApp calls. Destination IP addresses (WhatsApp/Meta servers), timestamps, data volumes, and cell IDs are all logged. Combined with WhatsApp's own preservation response to law enforcement requests (they preserve, even if not decrypting), IPDR builds a strong circumstantial case. Signal and encrypted apps are harder but investigators can still see that communication occurred, even if not what was said.
The evasion: Leaving one's primary phone at home during crime to create a false alibi, while carrying a burner phone.

The counter: The "home phone" creates an alibi — but the burner phone's activity at the crime scene can be found via tower dump. Additionally, behavioral pattern analysis (the home phone suddenly goes silent during the crime window, then resumes) is itself suspicious. Investigators look for phones that go dark exactly when crimes occur.
The evasion: Modifying the IMEI of a device to impersonate another device or display a random/invalid number.

The counter: IMEI tampering is a criminal offence under the Indian Wireless Telegraphy Act and now the Telecom Act 2023. Operators can detect duplicate IMEIs (same IMEI connecting in two geographically impossible locations simultaneously). CEIR flags tampered IMEIs. The forensic examination of a seized device reveals the hardware chip's authentic IMEI even if software-level spoofing was attempted.
The evasion: Using a SIM from a foreign country or a virtual number (Skype, Google Voice) to avoid Indian CDR.

The counter: International roaming SIMs still connect to Indian towers and their IMEI still appears in tower dumps. Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLATs) allow cross-border record sharing — slow but effective for serious crimes. Virtual numbers from platforms like Skype have subscriber records accessible through proper channels.
The evasion: Keeping the phone in airplane mode or a signal-blocking bag during the crime.

The counter: Forensic examination of the device shows a gap in connectivity logs. A phone that goes to airplane mode at 9:00 PM and returns at 11:30 PM on the night of the crime has an unexplained gap that corroborates other evidence. Modern forensic tools can also recover deleted call logs, network connection history, and GPS coordinates cached by apps.
Section 11

Common Myths — Debunked

✗ Myth
"Police can track your phone's GPS location in real-time."
✓ Reality
Police use tower-based location — approximate and not real-time. GPS data requires device access or court-ordered cooperation from the OS (Apple/Google), which is a significantly higher bar.
✗ Myth
"Deleting call logs protects you from CDR evidence."
✓ Reality
CDR is stored at the operator's server — your phone's call log is irrelevant. Deleting it from your device doesn't affect what the operator has logged.
✗ Myth
"WhatsApp calls are completely invisible to police."
✓ Reality
Content is encrypted, but IPDR shows timing, duration, cell ID, and that a data session to WhatsApp servers occurred. Metadata is very revealing.
✗ Myth
"Using a new SIM makes you untraceable."
✓ Reality
The handset IMEI still connects you to prior activity. All SIMs used in the same device appear in CDR as the same IMEI.
✗ Myth
"Police always know your exact address from your phone."
✓ Reality
Tower data gives a probable area. Your exact address comes from the SDR/CAF on your SIM registration — but even that may differ from your current location.
✗ Myth
"A switched-off phone gives no location data."
✓ Reality
Correct — while off, no new CDR is generated. But the last tower ping before power-off is recorded, and patterns around when a phone goes dark are forensically significant.

The Core Truth
Telecom evidence is almost never standalone. Its power is as a corroborating and narrowing tool — it tells investigators where to look and what to verify. The courtroom evidence is built through CCTV, witness testimony, device forensics, and physical evidence — with telecom data as the framework that holds it all together.
Final Thoughts

What This Means — For Investigators, Lawyers, and Citizens

India's telecom investigation infrastructure is powerful, evolving, and in some ways less regulated than those in Western democracies. For law enforcement, it is an increasingly essential tool for solving serious crimes. For civil liberties advocates, the lack of comprehensive oversight legislation — particularly around tower dumps and bulk interception — remains a legitimate and important concern.

For anyone studying digital forensics, cybersecurity, or law: understanding this ecosystem isn't optional. Increasingly, criminal cases — from street crime to financial fraud to terrorism — are built on telecom evidence. The ability to read a CDR, understand what a tower dump reveals, know what IPDR proves, and challenge a 65B certificate appropriately is becoming a core skill.

And for the rest of us: our phones generate a detailed, persistent record of where we are, who we talk to, and what services we use. That data is stored, it is accessible, and it is used. Knowing how is the beginning of informed participation in conversations about privacy, surveillance, and the state.
